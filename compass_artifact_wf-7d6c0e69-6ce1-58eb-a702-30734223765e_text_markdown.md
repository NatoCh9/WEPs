# Hero Sections & Copywriting Teardown: 8 Leading Sites for Corporate Engagement on Women's Causes

## TL;DR
- Across all 8 sites, hero copy splits into two dominant strategies: **movement/emotional invitation** (HeForShe, Vital Voices, Cherie Blair, IWD) versus **business-case/performance framing** (Catalyst, WEPs, 30% Club, Lean In Circles) — with Catalyst and Lean In most deliberately blending inspiration with hard ROI data.
- The most common CTA verbs are soft-commitment imperatives — "Join Us," "Become a Supporter," "Get Started," "Bring Circles to your company," "Donate" — and recurring keywords cluster tightly around *accelerate, empower, inclusion, parity, champions, impact, and equality*.
- Sites addressing companies/CEOs (Catalyst, WEPs, 30% Club, Lean In) lead with performance, talent, and ROI language; sites addressing individuals (HeForShe, IWD, Vital Voices, Cherie Blair) lead with solidarity, invitation, and human-story language.

## Key Findings
- **Hero format:** Video-carousel heroes dominate (HeForShe, WEPs, Catalyst, Vital Voices, 30% Club all rotate multiple slides). Cherie Blair and Lean In Circles use simpler single-message heroes; IWD uses a stacked banner grid.
- **Imagery:** Real-people photography is near-universal (HeForShe, Vital Voices, Cherie Blair, Lean In, Catalyst). 30% Club and IWD lean more on graphic/logo/illustration treatments; WEPs' hero is image-only promo banners with no headline text.
- **Numbers in heroes:** Catalyst, HeForShe, Vital Voices, Cherie Blair, and Lean In all place stat callouts at or just below the hero; 30% Club builds its entire identity around one number.
- **Tone:** HeForShe and Vital Voices are the most emotive/activist; Catalyst, 30% Club and WEPs the most corporate/data-driven; Cherie Blair, Lean In and IWD blend warmth with a business rationale.

## Details (site-by-site)

### 1. HeForShe (heforshe.org)
**Hero format & layout:** Full-bleed **rotating video carousel** (5 auto-playing MP4 slides), centered white text over motion footage. Each slide pairs a headline with a one-line subhead and a single CTA.

**Hero headline (rotating), verbatim examples:**
- "UK Policing renews HeForShe commitment for a further five years"
- "HeForShe Alliance Impact Report 2025"
- "From Conversation to Action"
- "Calling for Action Against Online Misogyny"

**Subheadlines (verbatim examples):** "building on measurable progress towards gender equality"; "With A Five-Point Plan for Change."

**CTA buttons:** One CTA per slide — "READ MORE" and "LEARN MORE." The persistent top-nav CTA is "Join Us." Verbs are a mix of soft ("Read More," "Learn More") and the site-wide "Join Us."

**Stat callouts (just below hero):** Three icon-based stats — "2 Million activists in our community and growing," "3 Billion conversations on social media," "600 Million citizens represented by our global partners."

**Secondary section copy:** Immediately below the carousel a mission banner reads: **"We Are The United Nations Global Solidarity Movement For Gender Equality."** Supporting copy: "HeForShe is an invitation for men and people of all genders to stand in solidarity with women to create a bold, visible and united force for gender equality." A later section titled "Be the Change" introduces Champions, Solutions and Resources.

**Tone & wording:** Warm, activist, invitational. Recurring keywords: *solidarity, invitation, movement, accelerate, champions, commitment, gender equality, action*. Corporate audience is addressed through the "Champions" language ("the most ambitious leaders from across government, business, non-profit, and academia"), while individuals get "supporters like you." Emma Watson's quote ("If not me, who? If not now, when?") anchors the emotional register.

### 2. Women's Empowerment Principles / WEPs (weps.org)
**Hero format & layout:** The top of the homepage is a **rotating image-only carousel** of ~5 promotional banners (events, WEPs Learn, Global Supply Chain Coalition). Critically, **the hero has no headline/H1 text** — the banners are clickable images only. The first on-page text heading appears below the carousel.

**First text heading (verbatim):** **"Explore. Engage. Empower"** — functioning as the de facto homepage tagline.

**Site-wide slogan:** "Equality Means Business" is WEPs' long-standing subtitle (used across brochures and the CEO Statement), though it does not currently render as visible hero text on the live homepage.

**CTAs:** Top-nav "Sign In" and "Join." On the Join page the H1 is "Join the WEPs" with the action framing "Apply to become a WEPs signatory"; the named sign-on link is "the CEO Statement of Support." Opening line of the Join page: "All businesses stand to benefit from gender equality and women's empowerment."

**Mission / about-in-one-sentence (verbatim, About page):** "The Women's Empowerment Principles (WEPs) are a set of Principles offering guidance to business on how to advance gender equality and women's empowerment in the workplace, marketplace and community."

**CEO-directed framing (verbatim):** "By joining the WEPs community, the CEO signals commitment to this agenda at the highest levels of the company." The Join page cites scale as "nearly 12,000 companies in 160 countries have adopted the WEPs" — this is WEPs' broadest self-reported adoption figure and is not independently audited; for context, the UN Global Compact's own take-action page cites "over 3,400 CEO-level signatories," so the headline count varies by how "signatory" is defined.

**Stat callouts:** No live hero counter; the homepage rotates individual signatory cards (Accor, Global Business Travel Association, etc.).

**Tone & wording:** Institutional, corporate, framework-oriented. Recurring keywords: *workplace, marketplace and community* (a signature triad), *signatory, commitment, gender-responsive, transparency, accountability, business case*. This is the most explicitly B2B/CEO-targeted of the eight — the audience is almost exclusively the signing company leader, not an individual.

### 3. Catalyst (catalyst.org)
**Hero format & layout:** **Rotating multi-slide carousel** with full-bleed photographic/graphic backgrounds, text-left. Each slide is a distinct campaign (Convene event, Convergent Leader toolkit, Trust Suite, Men at Work book, donation).

**Hero headlines (rotating, verbatim):** "Catalyst Convene New York"; "Make AI work for people and performance"; "When trust works, the business works"; "A roadmap to stronger performance"; "Amplify your impact."

**CTA buttons (verbatim):** Multiple, one per slide — "Learn more," "Get the toolkit," "Explore the Trust Suite," "Order on Amazon," "Donate today." Persistent CTA site-wide: "Become a Supporter."

**Secondary section (immediately below hero), verbatim:** "We accelerate performance and progress through workplace inclusion — for women and everyone." Followed by: **"Inclusion is a business imperative."** with four benefit cards: "Attract and retain talent," "Foster team innovation," "Build and sustain trust," "Drive sustainable business results."

**Stat callouts (verbatim):** "24M+ employees engaged worldwide," "180 countries represented," "500+ reports, tools, and solutions," and "Our 350+ member network." (These are organizational self-reports.)

**Tone & wording:** Authoritative, data-driven, corporate. This is the most business-case-forward site. Recurring keywords: *accelerate, performance, progress, inclusion, business imperative, trust, research-backed, solutions, impact*. The audience is explicitly the employer/leader ("Transform your culture with Catalyst"; "make inclusion your strategic advantage"). Notably reframes from "women" to "for women and everyone."

### 4. Vital Voices (vitalvoices.org)
**Hero format & layout:** **Rotating carousel of event/news slides**, each with a large headline, category label, supporting sentence, and multiple CTAs, paired with real photography (split text-left / image-right).

**Hero headlines (rotating, verbatim):** "A Powerful Night for Women's Leadership"; "Walking for Peace, Leading Change"; "8 Former Presidents and Prime Ministers join VV to Support My Voice My Choice."

**CTA buttons (verbatim):** Several per slide — "View Photo Gallery," "Livestream Here," "Meet the Honorees," "Step Into the Story," "Sign the Petition." Site nav CTA: "Donate."

**Secondary/mission statement (verbatim):** "Vital Voices Global Partnership is a non-profit founded on the simple idea that nations and communities cannot move forward without women's voices in leadership positions." Supporting line: "We invest in women leaders who are taking on the world's greatest challenges."

**Stat callouts (verbatim):** "3000000 — Vital Voices impacts 3 million people each year"; "28 [years] — In 1997, Vital Voices was created to make space for women to be heard"; "195 Participating Countries and Territories"; "47000+ Change Makers."

**Tone & wording:** Inspirational, prestige-driven, storytelling-led. Recurring keywords: *invest, leaders, changemakers, movement, transform, venture catalysts, daring, bold vision*. Corporate audience is handled on a separate "Corporate Alliance" track ("tailored engagement opportunities," "align their brand with a global movement"), while the homepage speaks to donors and supporters emotionally ("Join The Movement").

### 5. Cherie Blair Foundation for Women (cherieblairfoundation.org)
**Hero format & layout:** Single, focused hero — large headline over supporting sentence, real photography of a woman entrepreneur, clean single-message layout (not a heavy carousel).

**Hero headline (verbatim):** **"Together we are unstoppable"**

**Subheadline (verbatim):** "We empower women all over the world to start, sustain and grow successful businesses, so they can live to their full potential. Together, we are unstoppable."

**CTAs:** Nav-level "Donate"; in-body section CTAs "Discover our programmes," "Find out how," "Find out more," and a prominent "Donate now."

**Secondary section (verbatim):** Headline **"Our impact"** — "We work with partners to eliminate barriers to entrepreneurship for women, enabling global economic gender justice." With stat callouts: "300,000 — We've supported 300,000 women to start and grow successful businesses"; "79% of low & middle income countries served by our programmes"; "Millions of lives touched through the ripple effect."

**Mission (verbatim, About):** "Our mission is to empower women in low and middle income countries to start, sustain and grow successful businesses, and to build fair and inclusive business environments."

**Tone & wording:** Warm but economically framed — blends empowerment language with an entrepreneurship/economic-justice business rationale. Recurring keywords: *empower, entrepreneurs, start-sustain-grow, unstoppable, economic gender justice, potential, thrive*. Corporate audience gets a dedicated "Corporate and Institutional Partnerships" page ("Purpose-designed collaborations that advance women's entrepreneurship") with mutual-benefit framing ("enhancing talent pools, supply chains and markets").

### 6. Lean In / Circles for Companies (leanin.org/partner/company-circles)
**Hero format & layout:** Clean single-message hero, text-left with a grid of three real-people photos (workplace/mentoring scenes) at right.

**Hero headline (verbatim):** **"Empower your workforce"**

**Subheadline (verbatim):** "Help your organization maximize its performance by getting the best out of every employee. Circles for Companies are free, employee-led, self-organizing groups that meet regularly to build skills and support one another."

**CTA button (verbatim):** "Bring Circles to your company" (repeated as "Get Started" and "Get started" further down). Single primary hero CTA.

**Stat callout (immediately below hero, verbatim):** "Circles are effective — 94% of participants report a positive impact, such as stronger leadership skills, earning a raise or promotion, or feeling empowered to take on new challenges." The corporate landing page reinforces this with additional proof points: "98% of women in company Circles credit their Circle with positive changes," and for the sister program, "96% of managers" who complete a 100 Ways to Fight Bias workshop "feel better equipped to talk to their teams about bias."

**Secondary section (verbatim):** "A comprehensive leadership program" with four cards: "Leadership materials," "Online platform," "Flexible and scalable," "Free programming." Followed by "Trusted by thousands of companies" (Walmart, P&G, NBA, MetLife, Mattel logos). Lean In's broader scale, per its own reporting: more than 100,000 Circles in 183 countries, and a Women in the Workplace study (with McKinsey & Company) covering more than 1,000 companies and roughly 490,000 individuals over its 11-year history.

**Tone & wording:** Corporate, benefit-led, ROI-framed but warm. Recurring keywords: *empower, performance, leadership, skills, employee-led, free, scale, support*. This is the most explicitly B2B page in the Lean In ecosystem — addressed to HR/company leaders ("your organization," "your workforce," "no additional budget"), whereas the main leanin.org site speaks to individual women ("inspire and equip women to lead").

### 7. 30% Club (30percentclub.org)
**Hero format & layout:** Simple hero — a welcome headline over one supporting line, followed by a structured "Global mission" band built around the number 30% and three pillar icons.

**Hero headline (verbatim):** **"Welcome to the 30% Club"**

**Subheadline (verbatim):** "A business-led campaign to boost female representation at board and C-Suite level in the world's biggest companies."

**Secondary "Global mission" section (verbatim):** "At least 30% representation of all women on all boards and C-suites globally." With three strategic pillars: **"Activate"** (Activate Chairs & CEOs as members), **"Influence"** (Influence those with power to drive change), **"Enable"** (Enable future women leaders).

**CTAs:** "READ MORE" (repeated across About/Chapters/Our work sections) and nav-level "Join us" / "Support us." Softer, exploratory verbs dominate.

**Stat/number use:** The number "30%" is the entire brand hook; body copy adds "we've exceeded 40%" and "Path to Parity." The site's central claim — "Time and again research shows diverse companies outperform their less diverse peers" — echoes the widely cited McKinsey & Company evidence (its 2023 "Diversity matters even more" report found companies in the top quartile for executive-team gender diversity had a 39% greater likelihood of financial outperformance versus the bottom quartile, up from 15% in 2015). External validation of the campaign's progress narrative comes from the UK government-backed FTSE Women Leaders Review (Feb 2025), which found women hold about 43% of FTSE 350 board roles, up from roughly 9.5% in 2011.

**Tone & wording:** Corporate, boardroom, peer-to-peer among executives. Recurring keywords: *business-led, representation, boards, C-suite, parity, Chairs & CEOs, target, outperform*. The audience is unambiguously senior leaders; there is almost no individual-supporter emotional appeal — the argument is the business case for diversity plus voluntary CEO commitment.

### 8. International Women's Day (internationalwomensday.com)
**Hero format & layout:** A stacked grid of banner tiles (theme, event packs, giving, resources, events) rather than a single hero — each tile is a linked headline block over graphic imagery.

**Hero/lead headline (verbatim):** **"#IWD2026 | #GiveToGain"** with the campaign line "IWD 2026 campaign theme is GIVE TO GAIN."

**Signature aspirational statement (verbatim, appears prominently):** "Imagine a gender equal world. A world free of bias, stereotypes, and discrimination... Together we can forge gender equality."

**CTAs (verbatim):** Many parallel tiles — "GIVE TO GAIN," "Free IWD Resources & Toolkit," "SEARCH IWD EVENTS," plus "Register" / "Login/Join." Verbs blend imperative ("Search," "Register") with thematic ("Give to Gain").

**Secondary sections (verbatim):** "IWD FUNDRAISING — Plan and amplify your IWD Giving activity"; "GET INVOLVED WITH IWD — Collaboration is key to advancing gender equality"; "International Women's Day is powered by the collective efforts of all."

**Tone & wording:** Inclusive, celebratory, campaign-driven, broad. Recurring keywords: *forge, collective, inclusive, collaboration, celebrate, action, gender parity, together*. It addresses everyone simultaneously — "All IWD activity is valid" — with corporate engagement funneled through events, giving, and collaboration tiles rather than a distinct CEO pitch. (Note: internationalwomensday.com runs its own annual theme, "Give To Gain" for 2026, which is distinct from UN Women's separate official theme, "Rights. Justice. Action. For ALL Women and Girls.")

## Cross-site comparison: copywriting & tone strategies

**The emotional/inspirational pole:** HeForShe and Vital Voices sit furthest toward emotive, movement-building language. Both use "movement" and "solidarity/invest in women" framing, celebrity/leader quotes, and invitation verbs. Cherie Blair and IWD are also inspirational but temper it — Cherie Blair with an entrepreneurship/economic argument, IWD with a broad participatory "all activity is valid" inclusiveness.

**The data-driven/business-case pole:** Catalyst, 30% Club and WEPs are the most corporate and least sentimental. Catalyst leads with "Inclusion is a business imperative" and performance metrics; 30% Club reduces the entire cause to a boardroom target ("30% … on all boards and C-suites"); WEPs is a pure B2B framework speaking to the signing CEO ("Equality Means Business").

**The deliberate blenders:** **Catalyst** and **Lean In Circles** are the clearest hybrids — both wrap warm, human photography and empowerment vocabulary ("Empower your workforce," "for women and everyone") around hard ROI proof points (94% positive impact; 24M employees; retention/innovation cards). **Cherie Blair** blends emotional ("Together we are unstoppable") with economic ("global economic gender justice," "ripple effect"). These blended sites are arguably best-optimized for corporate engagement because they give a CEO both a mission to believe in and a number to justify the spend.

**Audience-switching in language:** The sharpest tonal shift by audience appears at Lean In (individual site = "inspire and equip women to lead"; company page = "maximize its performance," "no additional budget") and Vital Voices (homepage = "Join The Movement"; Corporate Alliance = "align their brand," "tailored engagement"). Catalyst and 30% Club barely switch at all — they are corporate-first throughout. HeForShe switches via labels: "Champions" for leaders, "supporters like you" for individuals.

**CTA patterns:** Imperative commands dominate but skew toward low-friction commitment. Hard-ask verbs ("Donate," "Join Us," "Sign," "Become a Supporter") appear on the mission/movement sites and membership orgs; softer discovery verbs ("Learn More," "Read More," "Explore," "Discover," "Find out how") dominate the exploratory carousels (HeForShe, Catalyst, 30% Club). Lean In and Cherie Blair use action-of-adoption verbs ("Bring Circles to your company," "Get Started," "Discover our programmes").

**Shared vocabulary:** Across all eight, the highest-frequency shared terms are *empower, accelerate, impact, inclusion, parity/equality, commitment, and champions/leaders*. "Accelerate" and "empower" are near-universal; "parity" recurs specifically on the business-case sites (Catalyst, 30% Club, WEPs, HeForShe).

## Recommendations
- **If building a corporate-engagement hero, blend the two poles like Catalyst/Lean In:** pair one emotional/aspirational line with one hard proof-point stat in or directly beneath the hero. This satisfies both the values-driven and ROI-driven decision-makers in a corporate buying committee. Threshold to change: if a values-only hero underperforms on qualified corporate leads, add a metric-led sub-band.
- **Match CTA verb friction to audience temperature:** use soft discovery verbs ("Explore," "Learn More," "Get the toolkit") for cold corporate traffic and reserve hard-ask verbs ("Become a Supporter," "Sign," "Donate") for warmed audiences or dedicated membership pages — mirroring how Catalyst and 30% Club stage their asks.
- **Lead with a signature number if you have one:** the 30% Club and Catalyst show that a single memorable metric (30%; 24M employees) is more sticky than prose. Benchmark to change course: if engagement/conversion on a prose hero underperforms, test a number-led variant.
- **Segment language by audience explicitly:** follow Lean In's and Vital Voices' model of a distinct corporate/partner page with performance-and-brand language separate from the individual-supporter movement language. If a company's leaders can't find a business rationale within one scroll, add benefit cards à la Catalyst's four-card "business imperative" band.
- **Use the "workplace, marketplace and community" triad or an equivalent memorable structure** (WEPs) to make abstract commitments concrete and repeatable across the site.

## Caveats
- Hero content on carousel-driven sites (HeForShe, WEPs, Catalyst, Vital Voices) **rotates and is time-sensitive** — the exact headlines captured here reflect the live state as of mid-2026 (e.g., IWD's "Give To Gain" 2026 theme, Catalyst's "Convergent Leader" campaign) and will change with campaigns.
- WEPs' hero is image-only; some banner text lives inside images that cannot be extracted as text, so WEPs' "headline" is represented by its first on-page heading ("Explore. Engage. Empower").
- Several stat figures are marketing self-reports (e.g., HeForShe "2 Million activists," Catalyst "24M+ employees engaged," Vital Voices "3 million people impacted," WEPs "nearly 12,000 companies") and should be treated as organizational claims, not independently audited numbers; where an external cross-check exists (e.g., FTSE Women Leaders Review for the 30% Club; McKinsey for the diversity-outperformance claim), it is noted.
- Quoted copy is kept short (well under 20 words) for fair-use; some slides contain additional copy not reproduced here.
